Colorizing the Prokudin-Gorskii Photo Collection

Overview

Prior to the Russian Revolution of 1917, Sergei Mikhailovich Prokudin-Gorskii (1863-1944) obtained permission from the Tzar to document his country's history by traveling across the Russian Empire taking thousands of color photographs. Though no way of printing color photos existed at the time, Prokudin-Gorskii believed that this technology would be developed in the future so that he could eventually render the present in color, so long as the appropriate visual information was captured. His idea was to record three exposures of every scene onto a glass plate using a red, green, and blue filter. These RGB glass plate negatives depicting the final years of the Russian Empire survived and were purchased in 1948 by the Library of Congress, which has since digitized them and made them available online. The goal is to process a selection of these digital copies and bring the corresponding color images to life.

Approach

Each digital image file includes three color channels arranged vertically, as on the original glass plates. The first step is to divide the image into three equal parts to roughly separate the red, green, and blue channels. Fixing the blue channel, one can align the red channel and the green channel to it. The simplest way to perform this channel alignment is to exhaustively search over a window of potential displacements and score each one according to an appropriate metric, keeping the best diplacement for that channel. In my implementation, I opted to use normalized cross-correlation (NCC) as the alignment metric since it performed well on virtually all of the provided images. This function is simply a dot product between two flattened and normalized image vectors which measures the "similarity" between two channels in terms of their positional pixel intensities. Because artifacts often appear at the edge of each channel due to the way in which the images were preserved, I crop each image by 20 percent of the height or width on each side before running the search algorithm in order to focus solely on the interior components of the image. This way, NCC results are more accurate and informative.

This technique worked well on the smaller JPEG inputs, but became too costly for the larger TIFF images. Thus, I implemented an alternative search algorithm based on image pyramids to reduce the search space and expedite execution times. An image pyramid is a sequential representation of an image at multiple scales, which in our case is adjusted by a factor of 2 at each level. The algorithm first involves recursively halving the resolution of the input image channel until it is small enough to allow exhaustive search to run in a reasonable amount of time. Once the coarse displacement is computed at the top of the pyramid, the function descends the pyramid, updating the displacement if needed by performing a search over a finer range on a version of the image with twice the resolution as the previous layer. Ultimately, the algorithm bubbles all the way up the call stack, at which point several layers of progressively finer updates have been applied to the coarse displacement, leaving us with a reasonable estimate in a fraction of the time of exhaustive search.

The only remaining step is to shift the red and green color channels by the optimal displacement values found by the search algorithm and then stack the adjusted color channels on top of each other in RGB order to produce a digital color image! In addition, however, I decided to crop every output image by a fixed 5 percent of the appropriate dimension on each side to remove some of the visual artifacts that the borders create.

White Balancing

In order to simulate a neutral illuminant, I first need to estimate the given illuminant to counteract it. I decided to operate under the gray-world assumption which speculates that the average color in every scene is gray. Concretely, this means that each color channel is equal in magnitude on average. To adjust the colors with respect to this new basis, I first compute the average pixel intensity across all pixels and channels as the illumination estimate. Then, I broadcast this estimate as a triple and divide it element-wise by the average (R, G, B) value that appears in the image to produce normalization scalars for each color channel. Multiplying each pixel in the image by these coefficients creates a white balancing effect that can be observed in the image comparisons below.

Contrasting

After performing white balancing, I decided to add some contrast to the photos as well. The way to enhance the contrast of an image is to broaden the dynamic range. Histogram equalization accomplishes this by spreading out the most frequent intensity values that appear in an image, therefore leveling out the intensities and flattening the histogram. In my implementation, I used an adaptive histogram equalization function that computes localized intensity histograms for the image rather than a global one. I chose a subtle clipping limit of 0.005 to augment the images without deep frying them. The results of this transformation can be observed in the following image comparisons.

Edge Detection

In the case of the image of Emir, I was not satisfied with the alignment of the color channels in the base implementation. Because of the vibrant array of colors present in the clothing he is wearing, the intensity values of the three color channels do not appear to match up very well. To remedy this, I decided to apply the Sobel operator to perform edge detection on each color channel, thus featurizing them before the alignment process.
